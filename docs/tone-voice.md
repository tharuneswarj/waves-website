# Brand Tone, Voice & Expression Guide

*Last updated: March 2026*

---

## Brand Voice in One Line

Waves sounds like a thoughtful designer who understands engineering, enjoys the process, and doesn't need to raise their voice.

---

## Core Personality

Waves is:

- Curious
- Intelligent
- Honest
- Detail-obsessed
- Calmly confident
- Slightly playful
- Technically rigorous

We are makers who think deeply.

We do not sound corporate.

We do not sound trendy.

We do not sound like a startup pitch deck.

---

## Tone Principles

### 1. Clarity Over Hype

Never exaggerate. If the work is strong, it doesn't need adjectives.

**Avoid:**
Revolutionary / Cutting-edge / Game-changing / Disrupting / Next-gen / World-class / Premium / Luxury / Bold statement piece

**Prefer:**
Iterated / Refined / Calibrated / Resolved / Considered / Structural / Intentional / Deliberate / Layered / Honest

---

### 2. Process Is the Story

We talk about how it was made, what changed during iteration, why a decision was taken, what the material does, how light behaves.

We don't talk about lifestyle aspirations, trend alignment, or vague inspiration.

**Bad:**
> Inspired by the beauty of nature.

**Better:**
> The curvature increases gradually to soften shadow density near the base.

---

### 3. Technical, But Human

We are allowed to use technical language. But always connect it to experience.

**Bad:**
> We reduced extrusion multiplier to 0.94.

**Better:**
> A slight reduction in extrusion creates a softer glow and more breathable texture.

Technical detail should feel thoughtful, not mechanical.

---

### 4. Subtle Wit Only

Waves humour is dry, restrained, and observant. It should feel like a quiet smile — not a punchline.

**Never:**
Meme humour / Sarcasm / Loud jokes / Internet slang / Excessive emoji

**Good examples:**

Packaging:
> Printed over 19 hours. Worth every layer.

Instagram:
> Still refining.

Email subject:
> It glows differently this time.

Product card:
> Designed slowly.

---

### 5. Short Sentences Carry Authority

Use rhythm. Break lines intentionally.

> Layer height changes diffusion.
>
> We adjusted it again.
>
> It was worth it.

This works across website, packaging, and social.

---

### 6. First Person — When and How

When writing as the studio (website, product pages, brand documents) — use **we**.

When the founder is writing personally (LinkedIn, long-form posts, founder notes) — use **I**. This is honest and human. Don't hide the fact that this is a small studio built by a specific person with specific skills.

A Waves LinkedIn post might begin:
> I adjusted the toolpath three times before the diffusion felt right. Here's what changed.

That voice is more credible than a faceless "we" for founder-led content.

---

## Voice Structure

Every piece of content should follow this structure:

1. Start with a clear statement
2. Explain the reasoning
3. Connect it to experience
4. End with a confident close

**Example — Product Description:**

Start:
> This lamp is printed using a custom toolpath strategy.

Explain:
> The extrusion pattern varies gradually, creating density shifts along the height.

Connect:
> This allows light to diffuse softly near the top and remain grounded at the base.

Close:
> Designed to sit quietly. Built to last.

## Context-Specific Guidelines

---

### Website — About, Product Pages, Installations

Tone: Measured / Intelligent / Structured / Calmly confident

Focus: Process / Craft / Engineering clarity / Material decisions / Longevity

Avoid: Marketing slogans / Dramatic storytelling / Excess emotion

**Example:**
> Each piece is developed through parametric modeling and iterative prototyping. Layer density is calibrated to control diffusion. Surfaces remain visible to honour the fabrication process.

---

### Instagram

Tone: Slightly lighter / Observant / Still intelligent

Format: Short lines / Controlled pacing / Minimal hashtags / No trend chasing

**Good captions:**

> 18 hours.
> 2 iterations.
> 1 softer glow.

—

> We adjusted the toolpath again.
> It mattered.

—

> Geometry decides the form.
> Light decides the mood.

**Never:**
- "Drop now 🔥"
- "You won't believe this"
- Trend-driven hooks

---

### Packaging Inserts / Cards

Tone: Minimal / Subtle wit / Process-focused

One or two lines maximum.

**Examples:**
> Printed slowly. Refined carefully.

> This lamp took time. We think you'll notice.

> Designed with code. Finished by hand.

---

### Email — Studio Introductions and Project Outreach

Tone: Direct / Honest / Peer-to-peer

Structure:
1. Who we are, briefly
2. What we've made (link to portfolio or specific work)
3. Why it might be relevant to their project
4. Clear, low-pressure next step

**Subject line examples:**
- Lighting for your current projects — Waves Company
- We make custom fabricated lighting. This might be relevant.
- A few things we've made recently.

Never sales-heavy. Never urgent. If the work is right for them, one clear email is enough.

---

### Email — Marketing / Product Updates

Tone: Conversational / Honest / Process-led

Structure:
1. What changed or what's new
2. Why it changed
3. What it improves
4. Clear CTA

**Subject line examples:**
- We refined it again.
- A new iteration.
- Light, tuned carefully.
- This one took longer.

Avoid: "Limited time!" / "Hurry!" / "Last chance!" / Aggressive urgency

---

### LinkedIn

Tone: Thoughtful / Reflective / First person

Content: Share lessons / Share fabrication insights / Share iteration stories / Share design reasoning

**Example:**
> During prototyping, we noticed uneven diffusion near the base. Increasing wall thickness by 0.6mm corrected the shadow density without affecting form. Small adjustments change atmosphere.

No self-congratulation. No "thrilled to announce" tone.

---

## Words That Belong to Waves

Use often:

Intentional / Resolved / Calibrated / Considered / Refined / Iterated / Structural / Layered / Measured / Tactile / Honest / Deliberate

---

## Words That Do Not Belong

Avoid:

Disrupt / Innovative (unless context demands) / Cutting-edge / Visionary / Premium / Luxury / Bold statement piece / Viral / Trendy

Waves is not loud.

Waves is deliberate.

---

## Emotional Register

Waves sits between architectural discipline and maker curiosity.

It should feel: Grounded / Calm / Invested / Slightly playful / Deeply thoughtful

Never: Aggressive / Overly poetic / Overly sentimental / Corporate / Bro-technical

---

## Formatting Rules

- Short paragraphs
- Spacing used intentionally
- Line breaks for emphasis
- No excessive exclamation marks
- Emoji only if absolutely necessary — which is rarely

The layout should feel like the product: structured, clean, intentional.

---

## Final Litmus Test

Before publishing anything, ask:

1. Does this sound like someone who has done the work?
2. Is this clear without exaggeration?
3. Are we showing process instead of claiming greatness?
4. Is the humour subtle?
5. Would this still feel appropriate five years from now?

If yes — it's on brand.
