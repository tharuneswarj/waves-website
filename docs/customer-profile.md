# Waves Company — Customer Profile

*Last updated: March 2026*

---

## Overview

Waves serves three distinct customer types. They differ in how they find us, how they decide, and what they need from the brand. Understanding each one separately is what allows us to communicate clearly — and avoid the trap of writing for everyone and reaching no one.

The near-term business is built on **B2B relationships**. The long-term brand is built by **D2C trust**. Both matter. Neither replaces the other.

---

## Customer Type 01 — The Design Professional

**Who they are**

Architects and interior designers who specify lighting as part of a larger project. They work across residential, commercial, hospitality, and retail spaces. They have taste, technical literacy, and a client to satisfy. They are not buying a lamp — they are solving a spatial problem.

**What they care about**

- Whether the piece works in the space, not just in isolation
- The ability to customise — dimensions, configuration, finish, scale
- Technical credibility — they want to understand how it's made and why it behaves the way it does
- A brand they can show their own client without hesitation
- Reliable execution and clear communication through the project

**How they find Waves**

Primarily through warm introduction — a colleague, a shared project, the Koodu Architecture relationship. Eventually through portfolio visibility, Instagram, and direct outreach. Word of mouth is the primary acquisition channel at this stage.

**How decisions are made**

The architect or designer drives the recommendation. They propose Waves to their client, explain the rationale, and manage the brief. The end client rarely engages directly with us. This means our job is to make the specifier confident — confident in the work, confident in the story, confident that Waves will deliver.

**What they need from us**

A portfolio they can share. A process they can explain. A team that responds clearly and executes reliably. The studio deck and project photography are the primary tools for this customer.

**Best fit project types**

All project types are relevant, but volume and repeatability sit in:

- **Boutique hospitality** — cafes, restaurants, boutique hotels. Multiple pieces per project. Atmosphere is the brief. Waves is a natural fit.
- **Retail spaces** — lighting as brand expression. Distinctive pieces that signal that the space was designed with care.
- **Corporate and office** — the Salem installation is the model here. Custom configurations, pendant clusters, spatial presence.
- **Residential interiors** — higher design involvement per project, strong portfolio value, client becomes a long-term relationship.

**Price sensitivity**

Low to moderate. The lamp is a line item in a larger project budget. What matters more is confidence in the outcome and clarity in the brief.

**Current pipeline stage**

This is the active revenue channel. Koodu Architecture provides warm introductions. Portfolio is being built project by project.

## Customer Type 02 — The Hospitality & Retail Operator

**Who they are**

Owners or creative directors of boutique hospitality businesses — cafes, restaurants, small hotels, curated retail stores. They may work with an interior designer or make decisions independently. They care deeply about how their space feels and what it communicates to the people who walk in.

**What they care about**

- Atmosphere. How the light changes the mood of the room.
- Distinctiveness. Something that isn't available in a catalogue anywhere else.
- Value at scale. If they're buying 8 to 20 pieces, the per-unit story needs to hold.
- Speed and reliability of production once the brief is agreed.

**How they find Waves**

Through designers who specify us, through Instagram and Pinterest, and eventually through direct referral from other hospitality operators. A single well-photographed installation in a known café or restaurant is the most powerful acquisition tool for this customer type.

**How decisions are made**

Often the owner decides, sometimes in consultation with a designer. The conversation starts with the visual — does this feel right for the space — and moves quickly to brief, customisation, and cost.

**What they need from us**

Strong photography of completed hospitality installations. Clear customisation options. Confident pricing for small batches. A fast, direct conversation without unnecessary process.

**Price sensitivity**

Moderate. They are investing in the space and understand quality costs. The per-unit price is less important than the total value the lighting adds to the environment.

**Current pipeline stage**

Emerging. No direct hospitality clients yet outside of what has come through Koodu. This channel opens meaningfully once one strong hospitality installation is photographed and visible.

## Customer Type 03 — The Design-Conscious Homeowner

**Who they are**

An individual renovating or furnishing their home with intention. Lives in a city — Chennai, Bangalore, Mumbai, Hyderabad. Works in a professional or creative field. Buys things slowly and deliberately. Owns objects that have a reason to exist. Notices proportion. Would rather have one considered lamp than three forgettable ones.

They are 28 to 45. They follow architects and designers on Instagram. They save things on Pinterest before they buy. They read the label. They ask how it's made.

**What they care about**

- That the object was designed with a real point of view, not assembled from trends
- The story behind it — who made it, how, and why
- That it will still feel right in five years
- That it's not something anyone else has

**How they find Waves**

Pinterest saves, Instagram, a friend's home, editorial coverage. This customer discovers us visually before they discover us verbally. The photograph comes first. The story earns the purchase.

**How decisions are made**

Independently. This is a considered purchase — not impulse, not immediate. They may save the product page for weeks before buying. What converts them is brand trust: enough evidence that Waves is real, that the quality matches the price, that the person behind it knows what they're doing.

**What they need from us**

A website and Instagram that tells the full story. Honest product photography. Clear process content — what goes into making this, how long it takes, why it costs what it costs. Reviews or evidence from real customers over time.

**Price sensitivity**

Moderate to high. Rs. 4,999 to Rs. 5,999 is not a casual purchase for this customer. It is within reach, but it requires justification. The brand narrative — the origin story, the process transparency, the photography — is what provides that justification. Without it, the price floats without context. With it, the price makes complete sense.

**Current pipeline stage**

Not yet active. This channel requires brand infrastructure that is still being built — the website, the Instagram presence, the project portfolio, the origin story in place. Target activation: 12 to 18 months from now, as B2B portfolio and social presence mature.

## How the Three Customers Connect

The B2B customer funds the business in the near term and builds the portfolio.

The portfolio gives the hospitality and retail operator confidence to specify Waves.

The installations become the photography and evidence that the design-conscious homeowner needs to trust the brand enough to buy.

Each customer type feeds the next. None of them can be skipped.

---

## What This Means for How We Communicate

**For the design professional** — lead with capability, process, and portfolio. Show the work. Explain the craft. Be a peer, not a vendor.

**For the hospitality operator** — lead with atmosphere and outcome. Show completed installations in real spaces. Make the customisation conversation easy.

**For the homeowner** — lead with the story and the object. Show the lamp in a real room. Tell them how it was made. Give them a reason to trust before they spend.

The brand voice stays consistent across all three. What changes is the emphasis.
